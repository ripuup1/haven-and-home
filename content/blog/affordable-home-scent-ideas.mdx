There's a reason real estate agents bake cookies before an open house. Scent is the most emotionally powerful of all the senses, and the way your home smells shapes how people (including you) feel when they walk through the door. The good news is you don't need a $65 candle to make your house smell incredible. These five products cover every room in your home for less than the cost of one fancy department store candle.

Reed diffusers are the set-it-and-forget-it option. You put the reeds in the oil, flip them once a week, and the scent lasts for about 3 months. No electricity, no flame, no maintenance. This set comes with a glass vessel that looks like actual decor (not a plastic bottle with a label), and the scent is clean and light, not the headache-inducing kind. It's perfect for bathrooms, entryways, and anywhere you want consistent background fragrance without having to remember to turn something on.

Linen spray is an underrated trick. A few spritzes on your pillows, couch cushions, or curtains before guests arrive makes the whole room smell like you just did laundry. This one uses a lavender-and-linen blend that's relaxing without being overpowering. It's also great on sheets before bed. The scent fades naturally over a few hours, so it doesn't build up or become cloying. One bottle lasts about 2 months with daily use. The trick is misting from about 12 inches away. Too close and you'll get wet spots.

Simmer pot sachets are the old-school method that's making a comeback. Drop one in a pot of water on the stove, bring it to a low simmer, and your kitchen fills with the smell of citrus, cinnamon, rosemary, or whatever blend you picked. It's like making your own Yankee Candle but better. These sachets come pre-mixed so you don't have to buy individual ingredients. Each one lasts about 2 to 3 simmering sessions (just add water and reheat). The downside is you can't leave the stove unattended, so it's best for when you're already in the kitchen cooking or cleaning.

An essential oil diffuser gives you the most control over your home's scent. You pick the oil, you control the intensity, and you can change the scent based on the season or your mood. This ceramic one runs quietly for up to 6 hours and has a soft ambient light that doubles as a nightlight. A few drops of eucalyptus in the bathroom, lavender in the bedroom, lemon in the kitchen. The oils do add cost over time, but a single bottle lasts months when you're only using 3 to 4 drops per session.

A wax warmer is the middle ground between a candle and a diffuser. It melts scented wax melts using a low-heat plate, releasing fragrance without smoke or soot. This starter kit comes with the warmer and a pack of wax melts to get you started. The melts are cheap to replace (usually $3 to $5 for a pack that lasts weeks), and you can mix scents by combining different melts. The warmer itself looks like a small ceramic lamp. It's plug-in, so no batteries to replace. Just swap the wax when the scent fades, which is usually after about 8 hours of warming.

The best approach is layering. A reed diffuser in the entryway for a constant welcome scent, a simmer pot when you're cooking to mask food smells, linen spray on the couch before guests sit down, and a diffuser in the bedroom for winding down at night. Total cost for all five: under $90. Your home will smell better than a spa.
