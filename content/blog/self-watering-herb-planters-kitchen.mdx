Fresh herbs on your kitchen windowsill sound lovely in theory, but most of us have killed at least one sad little basil plant that dried out between waterings. The problem is not that you are a bad plant parent — it is that herbs in small pots dry out faster than you expect, especially near a sunny window. Self-watering planters solve this by holding a reservoir of water that the plant draws from as it needs it, so you can go several days between fills without anything wilting.

A good windowsill herb planter should also have a clean, cohesive look — because unlike a garage shelf, your kitchen counter is somewhere you actually spend time and want things to look nice. Here are seven self-watering herb planters that work well and look good doing it.

## Quick Tips for Windowsill Herb Success

- **Use a south or west-facing window.** Most herbs need at least 6 hours of direct light daily. A north-facing window will leave you with leggy, flavorless plants no matter how good your planter is.
- **Do not overfill the reservoir.** Self-watering planters work best when you let the reservoir draw down before refilling. Keeping it constantly topped off can still cause root rot in some soil types.
- **Harvest frequently.** Pinching off leaves regularly encourages bushier growth. Letting a herb plant grow unchecked usually means it flowers faster and loses flavor sooner.
- **Start with easy herbs.** Basil, chives, and mint are the most forgiving on a windowsill. Cilantro and parsley take longer to establish. Hold off on rosemary and thyme until you have a feel for your window's light levels.
- **Label your planters.** Once herbs get established, they can look surprisingly similar. A small wood plant marker or a piece of masking tape saves the confusion of tasting test leaves to figure out which is which.
