Fresh herbs are one of the easiest ways to make home-cooked food taste more vibrant, but if you're pulling out a full cutting board and chef's knife every time you want to snip some chives or basil, you probably skip it more often than not. Herb scissors and dedicated herb tools remove that friction entirely. A quick snip directly into the pan or over the plate, and you're done.

The best part is that all of these tools cost under $15 — often under $10. They're impulse-buy territory, and most people who try multi-blade herb scissors genuinely never go back to knife-chopping fresh herbs. Here are six tools worth having if you cook with fresh herbs at all.

## Are Multi-Blade Herb Scissors Actually Better Than a Knife?

**For most herb-cutting tasks, yes — especially for soft herbs like chives, parsley, basil, and cilantro.** A 5-blade herb scissors cuts a handful of herbs into fine pieces in a single pass, which takes about 3 seconds and requires no cutting board. Cleaning them takes another 30 seconds. Compare that to pulling out a cutting board, knife, and dealing with the cleanup, and it's not even close.

Where a knife is still better: woody herbs like rosemary where you need to strip the leaves first, and situations where you want specific shapes (chiffonade, for example) that scissors can't replicate cleanly. For everything else, herb scissors win on speed and convenience.

## Quick Tips for Working with Fresh Herbs

- Cut herbs at an angle to expose more surface area — they absorb water better in a keeper
- Add delicate herbs (basil, parsley) at the end of cooking; add hardy herbs (rosemary, thyme) at the beginning
- Wrap herbs you are not keeping in a keeper in a barely damp paper towel and seal in a bag — they will last 3-5 days this way
- Herb scissors work best when herbs are dry — wet herbs clump and don't cut as cleanly
