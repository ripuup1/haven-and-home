Something weird has been happening in backyards this spring. Pizza ovens are everywhere. Not the giant brick-and-mortar built-ins your neighbor's father-in-law spent two summers laying. Compact, portable units that fit on a 24-inch table, hit 900°F in under 20 minutes, and pump out a charred Neapolitan pie in about 90 seconds.

I started noticing it around April. Three different friends posted pizza-night photos within a week. Then my Instagram algorithm gave up and started showing me nothing else. Turns out the home pizza oven category has quietly exploded in the last 18 months, partly because the prices finally dropped under $400 and partly because the smaller units actually work as well as the big ones for most home cooks. Here's what's worth buying, what to skip, and which one fits your specific patio situation.

## What to Look For

Before getting into specific picks, a few things matter more than the marketing copy suggests:

**Fuel type.** Gas ovens are easier (turn the knob, wait 15 minutes, cook). Wood-pellet and wood-fired ovens give you that real char-and-smoke flavor but require more babysitting. Multi-fuel models exist if you can't decide.

**Max temperature.** You want at least 900°F for true Neapolitan-style. Anything under 700°F gives you "good frozen-pizza" results, not "I just got back from Naples" results.

**Cook surface size.** Most compact units handle a 12-inch pizza. A few squeeze to 13 or 14. If you regularly cook for groups of six or more, the size jump matters. For a couple or family of four, 12 is plenty.

**Setup and storage.** "Compact" varies. Some weigh 22 pounds and fold flat. Others are 60 pounds and need a dedicated spot. Be honest with yourself about whether you'll wheel it out and back every Saturday or just leave it under a cover.

## How to Choose

If you're cooking for one or two people on a balcony, get the foldable model. If you're feeding a family of four on a normal patio, the budget propane oven covers 90 percent of pizza needs and saves you $200. If you're the type who reads pizza-making subreddits and wants to nerd out, the multi-fuel option is worth the upgrade. And if you want one thing that does pizza plus everything else, the versatile model earns its bigger price tag.

The biggest mistake I see is people buying based on max temperature alone. A 1000°F oven sounds impressive, but if you can't actually use it three times a month, it's a $600 paperweight.
