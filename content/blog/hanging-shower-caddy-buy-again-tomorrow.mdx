I've owned more shower caddies than I'd like to admit. Tension rods that slowly migrated down the corner over six months. Suction cups that gave out at 2 a.m. and dumped a full bottle of conditioner into the tub. Hanging caddies that rusted out near the screw holes and stained the tile. The category seems simple and somehow isn't.

The one I'd buy again tomorrow is the first one on this list. The other four are products I've tested in different bathrooms (including a tiny rental with a glass shower door and zero wall space) and would still recommend with caveats. Here's the honest breakdown.

## The One That Actually Earned Its Spot

The hanging caddy I keep coming back to is the over-the-showerhead style with stainless steel construction and proper drainage holes. The reason it works when others fail is boring: it doesn't try to do too much. No suction, no adhesive, no drilling. It just hangs from the shower arm and stays put.

I've had mine for 16 months. Zero rust, no sliding, holds two bottles of shampoo, conditioner, body wash, a face cleanser, and a razor without sagging. The trick I didn't know when I bought my first one: look for actual stainless steel, not chrome-plated metal. Chrome plating is what rusts. If the product page mentions "rustproof" without specifying SUS304 or 316 stainless, assume it'll fail in a year.

## The Adhesive Caddy That Doesn't Fall Off

If your shower has tile and you don't want to drill or use a hanging style, adhesive is the next best thing. The catch is that 80% of adhesive caddies on Amazon use cheap foam tape that fails after the first hot shower. The ones that work use a stronger pressure-sensitive adhesive that bonds to clean tile and actually holds 5+ pounds.

The install is the part most people get wrong. Clean the tile with rubbing alcohol, dry it completely, press the adhesive backing on for 30 full seconds, then wait 24 hours before adding any weight. If you load it up the same day you install it, it will fall. I've tested this in three bathrooms now and the adhesive holds when you actually follow the directions.

## The Tension-Pole Caddy That's Actually Stable

Tension poles get a bad reputation because most of them are flimsy and slide down over time. The good ones use rubberized tips, a thicker pole, and a spring tension mechanism that doesn't loosen. They're the right choice if your shower has a glass door or no available wall space.

Here's what nobody tells you about tension poles: they need to be installed against a hard ceiling. Drop ceilings or soft drywall let the tip slowly press in, which loosens the tension. If you have a textured drywall ceiling, put a small wood block at the top to spread the pressure. With that one fix, the pole stays up indefinitely.

## What I'd Buy First If I Were Starting Over

If I were furnishing a bathroom from scratch tomorrow, I'd start with the over-the-showerhead stainless steel caddy ($32) and stop there. It handles 90% of what most households actually need. If I had a glass-door shower or no shower arm, I'd go tension-pole instead. Everything else is supplementary.

The mistake I made early on was buying based on aesthetics first. The matte black caddy I bought three years ago looked great for two months and then started chipping where the spray-painted finish met the screws. Real stainless steel doesn't chip because the color is the metal itself. Spend the extra $5 to get actual stainless and you won't be replacing it.
