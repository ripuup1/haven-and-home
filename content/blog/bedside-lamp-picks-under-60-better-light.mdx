If your last bedside lamp hummed every time the dimmer was at half power, you already know what is wrong with most cheap bedside lamps. The bulb compatibility is mismatched, the wiring is undersized, and any LED bulb above 60 watts equivalent buzzes like a fluorescent in an empty office. The lamp itself is fine. The lamp at half-brightness is unusable.

I tested fourteen bedside lamps over the past two months. Some buzzed. Some flickered when they were almost off. A handful actually did the job — quiet at every brightness, dimmable without strobing, and built in a way that does not feel like the base will tip over the first time a cat brushes it. These are the seven that survived.

## Quick Tips

A few rules I have learned from testing fourteen lamps and replacing seven of them within a month.

- Use 2700K to 3000K LED bulbs for bedside reading. Daylight (5000K+) is too cold for evening rooms and makes everyone look tired.
- If a lamp buzzes on a dimmer, the bulb and dimmer are not matched. Switch to a dimmable LED labeled compatible with your dimmer brand. Or skip the dimmer with a three-way bulb.
- Felt bases matter more than people think. Without one, the lamp scratches the nightstand within a year.
- The cord should be at least seven feet for a bedside lamp. Six feet is just barely enough, and you will end up running it diagonally across the floor.
- Buy the bulb separately if the lamp comes with a basic incandescent. The included bulbs are almost always the cheapest, and a $4 LED upgrade transforms the lamp.

Bedside lamps are one of those purchases that looks simple and turns out to be full of small frustrations — buzz, flicker, wrong color temperature, cord too short, base too tippy. Picking the right lamp the first time saves you a return trip and a year of complaining about the buzz. These seven are quiet, they dim cleanly, and they look better than the price tag suggests.
