If you wake up stuffy, with itchy eyes, or a scratchy throat that clears up by mid-morning, your pillow is likely the culprit. Pillows accumulate dust mites — and more importantly, dust mite waste — at a rate that is genuinely unpleasant to think about. A pillow protector with a zippered encasement creates a physical barrier between you and whatever is living in your pillow, without changing how comfortable the pillow feels.

Here is the part that surprises most people: pillow protectors go under your pillowcase, not over it. The pillow protector encases the entire pillow (zipper closes it fully), and then your decorative pillowcase goes on top of that. You do not feel the protector at all when you are sleeping, but it blocks dust mite allergens from passing through to your face.

The key spec to look for is the fabric type and pore size. Tightly woven cotton or microfiber blocks allergens effectively. Fully zippered encasements work better than ones that just have a flap closure. All six options below zip shut, machine wash on a warm cycle, and stay under $20 for a standard/queen size.

## Quick Tips for Pillow Protectors and Allergen Control

- Wash pillow protectors every 2 to 4 weeks on the same cycle as your sheets for best allergen control
- Make sure the zipper is fully closed after putting the protector on — even a 1 inch gap can let allergens through over time
- If you are buying for young children, go with the waterproof version as the first layer
- Replace pillow protectors every 2 to 3 years even if they look fine — the tightly woven barrier fabric does degrade over time
- Use protectors on decorative pillows in the bedroom too, not just sleeping pillows, since they collect the same dust mite load
