Side sleepers are roughly 8x more likely to be woken up by a partner's snoring — the ear that's facing up is a perfectly aimed receiver. A $40 sound machine fixes most of it. Not all of it — if the snoring is loud enough to rattle the nightstand, you're in different territory — but light snoring and ambient noise disturbances are genuinely manageable with the right white noise setup.

The key is continuous, non-looping audio that covers the frequency range where snoring lives (200–900 Hz). Not all sound machines do this well. Here's what actually matters and what's worth buying.

## What to Look For in a Bedroom Sound Machine

- **True non-looping audio.** Machines that play a 30-second sample on repeat have an audible "click" or restart cue that can wake light sleepers. Look for fans, mechanical white noise generators, or machines that explicitly advertise non-looping playback.
- **Volume range.** You want a machine that goes loud enough to cover snoring without being so loud it becomes its own problem. 65–75 dB at full volume is the sweet spot.
- **Snore-specific sounds.** Pink noise and brown noise cover lower frequencies better than pure white noise and are more effective at masking snoring. A machine with multiple sound types gives you options.
- **Size and portability.** If you travel for work or want to use it in a hotel, a plug-in-only machine is annoying. Battery or USB-C machines travel well.
- **No light output.** Many sound machines have LED indicators or clock displays. If you're a light sleeper, a glowing device on the nightstand can be a problem on its own.

## How to Choose

If budget is the only constraint, any of the $20–30 machines with pink or brown noise options will handle light snoring. The step-up worth making is the Snooz if you're a true light sleeper or if the snoring is on the louder end of "light" — the real fan mechanism produces continuous, non-repeating audio that holds up to sustained listening in a way recordings don't.

Placement matters as much as the machine itself. Put it on the side of the bed closest to the snorer, at ear height if possible, or on a surface between the two of you. Putting it across the room halves its effectiveness.

If you or your partner run hot, a sound machine with a fan component does double duty as air movement. The Snooz is the best version of this — you get airflow, white noise, and snore coverage in one device that doesn't look clinical on the nightstand.
