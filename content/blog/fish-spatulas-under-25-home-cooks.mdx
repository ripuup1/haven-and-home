Here's a question worth asking: why does the spatula that cost $4 at a restaurant supply store work better than the $30 one from Williams-Sonoma? The answer is usually blade thickness. Most consumer spatulas are too thick to slide cleanly under a piece of fish without mangling it. A real fish spatula — thin, slightly flexible, slotted to drain fat — changes how you cook entirely. You'll reach for it for everything: eggs, pancakes, smash burgers, cookies off a baking sheet. Here are eight that do the job without breaking the budget.

## The Slotted Stainless Offset: The One Pros Use

**The stainless slotted offset fish spatula is the closest thing to a professional kitchen tool you can buy for under $15. The angled blade lets you get under food at a low angle, the slots drain grease, and the thin flexible edge doesn't tear fragile proteins.**

This is the spatula you see in every serious home cook's drawer and every restaurant line. The offset angle is the key feature — it keeps your hand above pan level so you're not burning your knuckles, and the thin beveled edge slides under a fish fillet without pushing it instead of lifting it. Look for one with a blade length of at least 5 inches and a handle that's comfortable enough to grip when your hand is slick from cooking fat.

## Quick Tips for Using Fish Spatulas

- **Let the food tell you when it's ready to flip.** A piece of fish that's properly seared will release from the pan on its own. If you have to force the spatula under it, wait another 30 seconds.
- **Thin blades work on more surfaces.** Your fish spatula is excellent for lifting baked goods off a sheet pan, scraping up fond in a stainless pan, and getting pastry dough off a countertop.
- **For nonstick pans, stick with silicone.** Even a careful metal edge will eventually scratch the coating. Keep one silicone turner specifically for nonstick cookware.
- **Don't store them blade-down in a crammed utensil drawer.** The thin edge bends if it's pressing against other tools at an angle. Hang them or keep them blade-up in a crock.
- **Hand wash anything with a plated or coated finish.** Dishwashers will strip copper plating and dull gold powder coat over time. Stainless and silicone are fine in the dishwasher.

The slotted stainless offset is the one to start with. Add a silicone version for nonstick pans, and you have everything you need. The copper, gold, and wood-handled versions are for when you want the drawer to look as good as the food tastes.
