Renting means the shower curtain situation you inherited is the shower curtain situation you have — unless you know what you&apos;re working with. Most apartment shower tension rods are either the $8 builder-grade chrome version that sags over time, or a previous tenant&apos;s fix that barely holds. Replacing it with a heavy-duty tension rod takes 10 minutes, costs under $40, costs you zero in security deposit damage, and is the single easiest bathroom upgrade available to renters. Here&apos;s the full setup, from rod to liner.

## The Problem: Your Rod Keeps Falling or Bowing in the Middle

The standard tension rod from the hardware store works fine for light curtains but bows or falls entirely once you add a heavier curtain or double it up with a liner. The fix is a heavy-duty tension rod with a larger diameter and stronger spring tension — specifically, look for a rod with a steel core rather than hollow aluminum, and end caps with rubber grips that actually hold against tile or drywall.

For extra-wide showers that exceed the standard 42–72 inch range, a curved rod in an extended size solves both the fit problem and adds the extra breathing room at the same time.

## The Problem: You Want a Curtain and a Liner on the Same Rod Without Sag

Running both a decorative shower curtain and a waterproof liner on one tension rod doubles the weight and can overwhelm a standard rod. A double curtain rod — two parallel tension rods mounted together — solves this cleanly: the liner hangs on the inner rod, the curtain hangs on the outer rod, and neither is fighting the other for space.

## The Problem: The Rod End Caps Slip on Smooth Tile

Some shower surrounds are extremely smooth tile or solid surface material, and even good rubber end caps slip after a few days. Non-slip end cap pads — small adhesive rubber circles that attach directly to the wall surface — give the tension rod end caps a textured surface to grip against without any permanent damage. ## What to Skip

**Skip the spring tension rods under $15.** They have thinner steel, weaker springs, and rubber caps that compress and lose grip within months. The $25–$40 range is where you get stainless construction and rubber caps that actually hold under a loaded rod.

**Skip chrome rings that aren&apos;t labeled rust-proof.** Even with a tension rod upgrade, rusted curtain rings create orange stains on your liner and look terrible. Stainless or plastic rings only.

**Skip plastic PEVA liners if mildew is a recurring problem.** Fabric liners that are machine washable break the cycle of replacing cheap plastic liners every few months. The upfront cost is higher; the annual cost is lower.

The full setup — heavy-duty tension rod + double rod if needed + stainless hooks + fabric liner — runs about $80–$100 and takes under 20 minutes to install. No tools, no holes, and all of it moves with you when you leave.
