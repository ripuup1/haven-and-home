Home oven pizza has a problem: the oven is too slow and the pan is too thin. By the time the top of a pizza finishes cooking, the bottom has been sitting on a cool surface and never crisped. A pizza stone or steel fixes this by storing heat and releasing it rapidly the moment dough makes contact — the same principle commercial deck ovens use. The result is a crust that's genuinely crisp on the bottom, not pale and floppy. Here's what actually works and which type is right for your setup.

## What to Look For Before You Buy

Before getting into specific picks, it's worth understanding the core tradeoffs so you know which category to shop in.

- **Thermal mass:** More mass = more stored heat = faster bottom crust browning. Steel wins here over stone, pound for pound.
- **Thermal conductivity:** Steel conducts heat faster than cordierite or ceramic stone. This is why baking steels produce a crispier crust in less time.
- **Thickness:** For stone, thicker (3/4" or more) = better heat retention and less risk of cracking from thermal shock. For steel, 1/4" to 3/8" is the sweet spot — beyond that it gets very heavy with diminishing returns.
- **Size:** Measure your oven rack. Many people buy a stone that's too wide and can't place it properly, blocking airflow around the edges.
- **Preheating time:** Both stones and steels need 45–60 minutes in a hot oven before you launch your pizza. This is non-negotiable.

## Best Budget: Cordierite Pizza Stone

**The best budget pizza stone is a cordierite stone in the 14–15 inch range. Cordierite is a naturally porous material that absorbs moisture from dough during baking (helping crispness) and handles thermal shock better than ceramic alternatives.**

Cordierite is what most commercial deck ovens are lined with. It's the material that handles sudden temperature changes without cracking, which matters if you ever put a cold or room-temperature pizza on a stone that's been preheating for an hour. Ceramic stones work but crack more easily under the same conditions.

## Best Steel: The Baking Steel

**The baking steel is the highest-performing option for home pizza, producing a crispier, more evenly browned bottom crust than any stone at the same thickness. The tradeoff is cost: a quality 1/4" baking steel runs $80–$120, compared to $30–$50 for a good stone.**

The physics favor steel. Steel has roughly 18 times the thermal conductivity of cordierite. When dough hits a hot steel, the heat transfer is so fast that the crust sets and browns before it has time to dry out the way it does on a slower-heating stone. The result is a crust that&apos;s crisp on the bottom but still has some chew — the same profile you get from a Neapolitan-style pizzeria.

## How to Choose: Stone vs. Steel

**If budget is the main constraint:** Go with a cordierite stone in the 14–15 inch range. The Unicook and Hans Grill options are both reliable, and a good stone will outlast your oven if you treat it properly (never wash with soap, avoid thermal shock from cold water on a hot stone).

**If you want the best crust possible:** Get a 1/4-inch baking steel. The performance difference is real and noticeable, especially if you&apos;re making pizza more than once a month.

**If your oven is small or you&apos;re in an apartment:** A 12-inch round stone fits in any oven and handles personal-size pizzas. Add the aluminum peel and you have a complete setup for under $65.

**For bread bakers too:** The rectangular cordierite stone does double duty for artisan bread. Bake sourdough boules and baguettes on it in addition to pizza, which makes the per-use cost drop fast.

The peel is the piece most people skip and then regret. Whatever surface you choose, add the perforated aluminum peel to the order. Your launch success rate will go from 70% to 98%.
