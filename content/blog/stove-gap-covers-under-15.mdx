If you've ever pulled your stove out from the wall, you already know the horror show that lives in that narrow gap between the range and the countertop. Rice grains, crumbs, sauce drips, the occasional piece of pasta that somehow launched itself sideways — it all ends up in that impossible-to-clean crevice. And once it's down there, it's basically archaeology.

Stove gap covers solve this in about thirty seconds. They're flexible silicone or rubber strips that wedge into that gap and create a seamless bridge between your countertop and stove. No more fishing for lost food with a butter knife. No more pulling the stove out every few months for a deep clean. Every option here is under $15, and most are under $12.

Here's what actually works and what to watch for before you buy.

## Do Silicone Stove Gap Covers Actually Stay in Place?

**Yes, but fit matters more than anything else.** The standard gap between a stove and countertop is about half an inch to an inch wide. Most silicone gap covers are designed to flex and compress to fill that range. The key is getting one that's slightly wider than your gap so it wedges in with gentle pressure rather than sitting loosely on top.

The T-shaped designs tend to stay put better than flat strips because the vertical fin tucks down into the gap while the horizontal part covers it. If your gap is wider than an inch, look for an adjustable or extra-wide option instead of trying to force a standard cover to span the distance.

## T-Shaped Silicone Stove Gap Filler: Best for Wide Gaps

**If your gap is closer to an inch wide and flat covers keep sliding down, the T-shaped design is a much better solution.**

The vertical fin drops into the gap and acts as an anchor while the flat top covers the opening. This two-part geometry means it holds position even when you bump the stove or slide a heavy pot across the surface. The silicone is food-grade and heat resistant, so there's no off-gassing concern. Most T-shaped fillers come in sets of two and run about $11 to $13 for the pair.

The only downside is that T-shaped covers are slightly more visible than flat ones — you can see the raised profile if you look closely. But if your flat cover keeps falling in, that's a worthwhile tradeoff.

## Extra-Wide Silicone Stove Gap Filler: Best for Oversized Gaps

**If your gap is wider than an inch — common with older stoves or non-standard counter depths — you need an extra-wide cover instead of stretching a standard one.**

Standard gap covers max out at about an inch of coverage. If your gap is 1.5 inches or wider, those standard strips either won't reach or they'll sag into the gap under their own weight. Extra-wide versions are designed for gaps up to 2 inches and use thicker silicone to span the distance without drooping. They cost about $13 for a pair and solve a problem that standard covers simply can't address.

Measure your gap before ordering. If it's under an inch, a standard cover is fine. If it's over an inch, go wide.

## Quick Tips

- Measure your gap width before ordering — most covers fit 0.5 to 1 inch, but wider gaps need extra-wide versions
- Cut from the back end (closest to the wall) so the visible front edge stays factory-clean
- Wash your gap covers monthly with warm soapy water to prevent grease buildup
- Replace clear silicone covers annually if they start to yellow from heat exposure
- If your stove is stainless steel, try the magnetic version first — the grip is noticeably better
